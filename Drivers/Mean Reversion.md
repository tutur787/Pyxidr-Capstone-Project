# Mean Reversion in Fixed Income

---

## What is Mean Reversion?

Mean reversion is the empirical tendency of interest rates to return to a
long-term equilibrium level over time. Unlike stock prices, interest rates
cannot rise or fall indefinitely:

- **Very high rates** suppress economic activity → central banks cut rates
- **Very low rates** stimulate borrowing → inflation builds → rates rise

This creates a natural gravitational pull toward a long-run mean level $\theta$.

> **Key insight:** Mean reversion is what makes interest rate models
> fundamentally different from stock price models (e.g. Black-Scholes),
> which assume no such pull.

---

## 1. Why Mean Reversion Matters for FABN

In the FABN context, mean reversion is central to **both sides** of the
balance sheet:

### Asset Side (C1)

- Bond coupons are fixed at issuance and do not change over time. Therefore, mean reversion does **not** apply to the coupon itself.  
- Cash flows generated by the bond portfolio must be reinvested over time. These **reinvestment rates are stochastic** and evolve with market conditions.  
- Mean reversion governs how these reinvestment rates converge toward long-term equilibrium levels.

### Liability Side (C3)

- The FABN crediting rate is fixed and must be paid regardless of market conditions. Therefore, mean reversion does **not** apply to the liability cash flows directly.  
- When evaluating future funding gaps or deficits, discount rates must be applied. These **discount rates are stochastic** and depend on the evolution of interest rates.  
- Mean reversion determines the path of these rates over time and therefore impacts the valuation of liabilities.

> **Key Insight:** Mean reversion applies to **market-driven variables** (interest rates, spreads, reinvestment rates),  
> but not to **contractual cash flows** (bond coupons or FABN crediting rates).
> If rates mean revert quickly, reinvestment risk is lower. If they are slow
> to revert, duration mismatches between assets and liabilities persist longer
> — increasing C3 capital requirements.

## 2. Mathematical Representation

### Cox-Ingersoll-Ross (CIR) Model

The general form of a mean-reverting stochastic process is:

$$dr_t = \kappa(\theta - r_t)\,dt + \sigma \sqrt{r_t}\, dW_t$$

Where:

- $r_t$ = short-term interest rate at time $t$
- $\kappa$ = speed of mean reversion (how fast rates return to $\theta$)
- $\theta$ = long-run equilibrium level (the "mean" rates revert to)
- $\sigma$ = volatility of interest rate shocks
- $dr_t$ = infinitesimal change in the interest rate (i.e., how much the rate moves over a small time step)  
- $dt$ = small increment of time  
- $dW_t$ = random shock (Brownian motion), capturing unpredictable market movements, where $dW_t \sim \mathcal{N}(0, dt)$  

**Interpretation of the drift term $(\theta - r_t)$:**

| Condition | Drift | Effect |
|-----------|-------|--------|
| $r_t > \theta$ (rate above mean) | Negative | Rate pulled downward |
| $r_t < \theta$ (rate below mean) | Positive | Rate pulled upward |
| $r_t = \theta$ (rate at mean) | Zero | No pull |

---

## 3. Key Parameters and Their Interpretation

| Parameter | Symbol | Typical Range | Interpretation |
|-----------|--------|---------------|----------------|
| Speed of mean reversion | $\kappa$ | 0.15 – 0.25 per year | How fast rates return to $\theta$ |
| Long-run mean (MRP) | $\theta$ | Updated annually by NAIC | The equilibrium rate level |
| Volatility | $\sigma$ | 1% – 3% per year | Magnitude of random shocks |
| Initial rate | $r_0$ | Current 3-month Treasury | Starting point for scenarios |

### Half-life of mean reversion

The time it takes for rates to travel halfway back to $\theta$:

$$t_{1/2} = \frac{\ln 2}{\kappa}$$

| $\kappa$ | Half-life |
|----------|-----------|
| 0.10 | 6.9 years |
| 0.15 | 4.6 years |
| 0.25 | 2.8 years |
| 0.50 | 1.4 years |

A $\kappa$ of 0.15–0.25 implies rates take roughly **3–5
years** to move halfway back to equilibrium.

---

## 4. Connection to the FABN

**In C3 (liability side):**
The CIR model generates the 50 interest rate scenarios used for cash flow
testing. The speed $\kappa$ and long-run mean $\theta$ determine how
extreme and persistent rate movements can be directly affecting the value of the liabilities (FABN)
and therefore C3 capital requirements.

**In C1 (asset side):**
Mean reversion affects reinvestment rates. When bonds mature, proceeds
are reinvested at prevailing rates. If rates have mean-reverted toward
$\theta$, reinvestment income is more predictable.

**In the optimizer (C3 constraint):**
The duration mismatch constraint $|D_A(w) - D_L| \leq \epsilon$ implicitly
relies on the assumption that rates will mean revert. If they did not,
duration matching would be insufficient to control interest rate risk.
